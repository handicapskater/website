Evidence that prosthetic skates are a mobility issue

This page summarizes administrative findings, court access records, DMV records, travel disputes, and public burden without treating uncertain legal questions as settled law.

IRS

Medical and tax framing

Skates are documented as functional disability equipment, not recreation. This supports the public argument that inline skates can be a non-traditional mobility aid.

DOT / FTA / BART

2007 FTA Letter of Finding

The FTA analyzed whether roller skates used as a mobility aid could be excluded as a direct threat. It concluded BART had not demonstrated direct threat in concourse areas and outside controlled areas, and stated BART must allow use there, subject to reasonable fact-based restrictions.

This does not replace a court ruling, but it is a federal civil-rights determination and should be treated as major administrative evidence.

DMV

Driving with skates

The DMV classified my disability through restriction code 50. The code was hidden and opaque: third parties had to contact DMV to understand what it meant.

That lack of transparency limited my ability to renew and use the accommodation. It was an opaque and inadequate accommodation, not a complete solution.

Motorcycle use with skates arose because public transportation refused access to the mobility aid. That history supports the need for transparent, functional recognition of prosthetic skates as a non-traditional mobility aid.

Air travel

Stranded far from home

The issue was not merely refusal to board. United Airlines flew me back east, then left me stranded approximately 3,000 miles from home by refusing to allow me to return with my prosthetic skates/mobility aid.

Read airline article
Public burden

Federal buildings and events

I was forced to remove my prosthetic skates to enter a federal building to file a systemic discrimination complaint. I was later again refused access when trying to meet with a pro bono attorney in a federal building.

The burden is simple: the system required painful walking in order to complain about being forced to walk painfully.

The same transportation history explains the motorcycle adaptation: when public transportation blocked the accessible path, I had to build another way to reach work, appointments, and public life.

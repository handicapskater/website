A 30-year fight to move through the world

The core story is simple: walking hurts, skating helps, and public systems repeatedly treat a medical adaptation like misconduct instead of access.

Pelvic injury and adaptation

Multiple pelvic injuries and sacroiliac dysfunction made ordinary walking painful. Inline skates became the practical adaptation because they reduce repeated impact and allow movement over distance.

Biomechanics case

The movement argument became technical: walking is ballistic and vertically loaded; skating is rolling, smoother, and less punishing for this disability.

Biomechanics basics | Walking | Skating

Transit and public access

CalTrain, BART, airports, bridges, events, and public agencies became recurring sites of accommodation fights. Videos and agency records document both denial and successful access.

Courtroom access

Court access matters because judges and public officials need a plain record: this is a mobility aid, not a stunt. Court orders and pleadings remain available in the legal archive.

Driving and work

Skates make driving and work possible by allowing safe transfer, standing, movement, and motorcycle operation. DMV records and employment accommodation materials show why the distinction matters.

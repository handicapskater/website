A Disability Nobody Recognized Until I Proved It

For more than 30 years, I have had to prove that walking causes pain and that skating gives me mobility.

The story in plain English

I have had to prove, again and again, that inline skates are not recreation for me. They are a non-traditional mobility aid and prosthetic adaptation that reduce the pain burden of walking.

A. The injury

A severe pelvic injury made ordinary walking painful and physically costly. Walking is not just uncomfortable. It can create pain, stress, and a physical price that accumulates with every required step.

The legacy injury pages preserve the medical and biomechanics detail for readers who need the deeper record.

Legacy injury background | Pain and physiological stress

B. Discovering skates as mobility

Inline skates became practical because they let me move with less painful walking impact. They shift travel toward smoother, more horizontal movement instead of repeated vertical impact.

For me, prosthetic skates are a functional mobility tool: they help me work, travel, drive, and access public life.

Legacy skating page | Why I ride a motorcycle with skates

C. Scientific support

The public story is supported by biomechanics research. Andrew Mahar, Timothy Derrick, Joseph Hamill, and Graham Caldwell studied impact shock and attenuation during in-line skating in Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise. I reference that work here in plain English as Inline Skating: An Analysis of Impact Attenuation.

The study supports the general biomechanical point that inline skating can reduce impact compared with higher-impact locomotion such as running. My case applies that idea to disability: reducing impact can be the difference between being trapped and being mobile.

D. The 2007 DOT/FTA determination

In 2007, the Federal Transit Administration published a civil-rights determination involving my complaint against BART. The FTA recognized the issue as involving roller skates used as a mobility aid.

The FTA analyzed reasonable modification and direct threat. It concluded that BART had not demonstrated a direct threat in station concourses or outside controlled areas, and stated BART must allow use there, subject to reasonable fact-based restrictions.

This is an FTA Letter of Finding and administrative determination. It supports the argument, but it is not a binding court precedent.

E. Driving with skates

The legacy homepage explains that the DMV classified my disability with restriction code 50. That code required someone to contact the DMV to understand what the restriction meant.

That hidden restriction was not a meaningful or transparent accommodation. It created practical barriers to renewal, travel, and spontaneous proof that I can drive and move with prosthetic skates.

F. Air travel

The air travel issue was not merely refusal to board. United Airlines transported me back east, then left me stranded approximately 3,000 miles from home by refusing to allow me to return with my prosthetic skates/mobility aid.

That experience shows why a non-traditional mobility aid needs clear recognition across travel systems before a person is far from home and out of options.

Air travel article | SFO approval document

G. Federal building access

I was forced to remove my prosthetic skates to enter a federal building to file a systemic discrimination complaint against the very actors involved in the discrimination.

Later, I was again denied access when trying to meet with a pro bono attorney in a federal building. The burden is simple: the system required painful walking in order to complain about being forced to walk painfully.

H. Wearable data and health AI

WHOOP, Polar H10, Kubios, Strava, and related metrics now make the physical cost visible. The signals include heart rate, HRV/RMSSD, duration, vertical/horizontal dynamics, impact burden, and activity cohorts.

This matters because disability is often invisible until data makes the burden visible. Health AI can help compare walking, skating, motorcycle travel, ParaTransit, wheelchair use, and public-access stress.

I. The present appeal

The Ninth Circuit appeal, and possibly Supreme Court review, is about whether disability law can recognize functional mobility rather than forcing disabled people into old categories.

The question is larger than one person or one pair of skates. Can the law recognize what actually lets a disabled person move through the world?

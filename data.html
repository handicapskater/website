Mobility Burden Dashboard

Wearable data shows why skating is my mobility aid, not recreation.

Walking is not simply low activity for me. Walking causes pain and physiological stress. Skating is a non-traditional mobility aid that lets me move farther with less systemic burden.

Why this dashboard exists

I built this dashboard to show the physical difference between painful walking, adaptive skating, motorcycle travel, wheelchair use, ParaTransit, and other mobility contexts. The goal is simple: make an invisible disability measurable.

Burden

Walking creates burden

For my pelvic disability, walking can create pain and cardiovascular stress even when distance is short.

Function

Skating restores mobility

Inline skates allow smoother, more horizontal movement, reducing the painful impact pattern that makes walking difficult.

Access

Forced alternatives can harm

When public systems deny skates, the alternatives can include painful walking, unstable ParaTransit rides, or unnecessary wheelchair use.

Wearables

Wearables can reveal access barriers

Heart rate, HRV/RMSSD, duration, activity cohorts, and movement dynamics can show when mobility becomes harmful.

How to read this dashboard

Start by comparing walking and skating. Distance alone does not tell the story. The key question is how much physiological cost is required to move. A short painful walk can impose more burden than a longer skate because skating changes the mechanics of movement.

Distance How far I can move in each mobility mode.
Duration How long the burden lasts before the activity ends.
Heart rate Cardiovascular load during movement, transit, or forced alternatives.
HR zones Time spent in higher exertion or stress zones.
HRV/RMSSD Recovery and autonomic regulation where available.
Activity category Skating, walking, wheelchair, motorcycle, ParaTransit, commuting, and related cohorts.
Movement pattern Vertical versus horizontal dynamics where available.

Key findings

The dashboard is evidence, not a standalone medical diagnosis or legal ruling. The pattern matters because mobility aids should be evaluated by function, not appearance.

  • Skating supports longer mobility with a more sustainable burden pattern.
  • Walking can produce disproportionate physiological burden relative to distance.
  • ParaTransit and forced alternatives may create passive stress, especially when rides are long, unstable, or mechanically harsh.
  • The data supports the need to evaluate mobility aids by function, not appearance.
  • This is a real-world wearable-health AI case study.

Why this matters to Google Fitbit and Health teams

Most wearables count activity. This project asks a harder question: is the movement healthy, harmful, accessible, or forced? HandicapSkater uses real-world biometric and mobility data to show how wearable analytics could detect adaptive mobility, pain-induced cardiovascular burden, and harmful transportation conditions.

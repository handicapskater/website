Employer case study

Health AI for disability that does not fit the default model

Fitbit-style products are powerful when they understand real bodies in real environments. This case asks whether wearables can detect when a system is forcing a disabled person into a harmful mobility mode.

Problem

Step counts and generic activity categories can misread disability. For me, walking can be the harmful activity, while skating can be the safer mobility aid.

Opportunity

Wearables can combine HR, HRV/RMSSD, route context, movement smoothness, activity type, recovery, and self-report to identify safer mobility patterns.

What I bring to a health AI team

This portfolio is not a polished lab dataset. It is a hard, high-value product problem with legal, medical, human, and model-design consequences.

Data science

Feature framing, activity comparison, time-series reasoning, wearable data interpretation, dashboards, and explainable health signals.

Accessibility insight

Lived domain expertise in ADA access, non-standard mobility aids, transportation burden, and invisible physiological stress.

Product judgment

Understanding how a health product can help users document needs while protecting privacy and avoiding one-size-fits-all fitness assumptions.

Candidate projects

  • Classify walking, skating, driving transfer, manual labor, recovery, and transit burden from wearable and route data.
  • Detect pain-induced cardiovascular stress using HR, HRV/RMSSD, recovery, and contextual labels.
  • Build privacy-preserving evidence summaries that a user could share with a clinician, judge, employer, or transit agency.
  • Use FSI as a protected, patent-pending stability signal while exposing only public-facing interpretation.
  • Design a disability-aware mobility score that values safe function over step-count volume.
